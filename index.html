The Limping Child

Assessment & Management in the Emergency Department

For UK Emergency Medicine Doctors

Introduction

A limp is an asymmetric gait and is a common paediatric presentation to the Emergency Department. The causes are numerous, ranging from the benign to the life-or-limb-threatening. A structured approach, considering the child's age, is crucial to identify serious pathology while avoiding unnecessary investigations.

Urgent: Identify Red Flags First!

Systemic Symptoms

  • Fever >38.5°C
  • Night sweats, weight loss, anorexia
  • Pallor, easy bruising, generalised lymphadenopathy

Pain Characteristics

  • Severe, constant, unrelenting pain
  • Night pain waking the child from sleep
  • Back pain

Clinical Signs

  • Non-weight bearing on a limb
  • Joint swelling, erythema, warmth
  • Bony tenderness
  • Neurological signs (weakness, sensory loss, abnormal reflexes)

History Clues

  • History of significant trauma
  • Recent travel abroad
  • Suspicion of Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)

Presence of any red flag requires senior review and urgent investigation. Consider sepsis, septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, malignancy (leukaemia, bone tumours), or NAI.

Age-Related Differential Diagnosis

Toddler (1-3 years)

  • Common: Toddler's FractureUndisplaced spiral fracture of the tibia. Often no clear history of trauma., Transient Synovitis
  • Serious: Septic Arthritis, Osteomyelitis, NAI
  • Other: Footwear issues, foreign body in foot

Child (4-10 years)

  • Common: Transient Synovitis, Perthes' DiseaseAvascular necrosis of the femoral head. More common in boys aged 4-8.
  • Serious: Septic Arthritis, Osteomyelitis, Malignancy
  • Other: Trauma (fractures, soft tissue), Viral myositis

Adolescent (11-16 years)

  • Common: SUFESlipped Upper Femoral Epiphysis. Femoral head slips at the growth plate. Can present with hip, groin, or knee pain., Osgood-Schlatter, Trauma
  • Serious: Osteomyelitis, Malignancy (osteosarcoma)
  • Other: Overuse injuries, Patellofemoral pain

Clinical Pathway

1. Focused History

Pain: Site, onset, duration, character, relieving/exacerbating factors. Systemic: Fever, malaise, weight loss. Trauma: Any injury, even minor? Birth/Developmental Hx. Recent infections? (esp. URTI)

2. Examination

"Observe, Palpate, Move" - Examine from the "toe to the spine".

  • Gait: Observe child walking/running if possible.
  • Look: Swelling, erythema, deformity, bruising, muscle wasting.
  • Feel: Temperature, bony/soft tissue tenderness, joint effusion.
  • Move: Assess active and passive range of motion of all joints in lower limbs and spine. Pay close attention to the hip (log roll).
  • Special Tests: As indicated (e.g., for knee/ankle stability).

3. The Afebrile, Well Child

No red flags, afebrile, systemically well, fully weight-bearing (may have a limp).

Consider benign causes. Investigations may not be required initially.

  • X-ray if: bony tenderness, history of trauma, or suspicion of Perthes'/SUFE.
  • Diagnosis often Transient Synovitis: Diagnosis of exclusion. Analgesia (NSAIDs), advise rest.
  • Safety Netting is KEY: Provide clear advice on when to return (worsening pain, fever, becoming unwell, not improving in 48-72 hours). Arrange GP follow-up.

4. The Febrile or Unwell Child (or Red Flags)

Fever, non-weight bearing, severe pain, or other red flags present.

This is an emergency. Requires senior input and urgent investigation.

  • Bloods: FBC, CRP, ESR, Blood Culture.
  • Imaging: X-ray of the affected area (and pelvis). Ultrasound may show effusion. MRI is definitive for osteomyelitis.
  • Orthopaedic Referral: Urgent discussion with the on-call orthopaedic team is mandatory. The child may need joint aspiration or surgical washout.
  • Admit: These children require admission for IV antibiotics and further management.
